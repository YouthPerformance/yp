## The "Rest & Ice" Myth

If you go to a general doctor with Sever's Disease, they'll likely tell you to "rest for 6 weeks and ice it." But here's what happens: your child rests, the pain goes away, they return to basketball—and the pain comes back immediately.

Why? **Because the tendon is still weak.**

Rest doesn't fix the underlying issue. It just temporarily removes the stress. The moment that stress returns, so does the pain.

## What Actually Works

James Scott's protocol focuses on **building tendon tolerance** without the impact of jumping and running. The key is isometric loading—holding positions that stress the muscle-tendon unit without movement.

### Why Isometrics Work

1. **Analgesic Effect**: Isometric holds literally reduce pain signals from the tendon
2. **Maintain Strength**: Unlike rest, you don't lose muscle
3. **No Impact**: Loading without jumping means no stress on the growth plate
4. **Progressive Overload**: You can gradually increase hold times as tolerance builds

## The Protocol

### Step 1: Kill the Pain (Isometrics)

The **Floating Heel** exercise creates an immediate pain-reduction effect. Do this every morning before activity:

- Stand on a step with heels hanging off
- Lower heels 1 inch below step level
- HOLD for 45 seconds
- 3 sets, daily

### Step 2: Build Tolerance

Once pain is manageable (below 3/10), progress to:
- Soleus squat holds (bent knee heel raises)
- Single-leg balance variations
- Wall lean isometrics

### Step 3: Return to Play

Sever's doesn't mean "no basketball." It means "smart basketball":
- Perform the protocol BEFORE practice
- Modify jumping drills temporarily
- Monitor pain levels daily

## When to See a Doctor

See a healthcare provider immediately if:
- Pain is severe (above 5/10) even at rest
- There's visible swelling or bruising
- Your child is limping or can't walk normally
- Pain doesn't improve after 2 weeks of the protocol
